  1. How does Padaco discriminate activity states such as sleep, wake, and non-wear?

Padaco uses Actigraph’s proprietary count output of the vector magnitude from all three accelerometers (i.e. x, y, and z-axis) in classifying the wearer’s activity state at any point in time during the study.  This is done by first digitally filtering the input data (i.e. the count signal) and then applying a series of classification rules.  Figure 1 contains a screenshot of Padaco’s single study mode summary window, and shows activity classifications for one subject.  A description of how these activity categories are defined using the the long sum and short sum filter results is explained next.

Activity is first broadly split between two general categories: (a) wear - the device is being worn, and is on the subject, or (b) non-wear - the device is not being worn by the subject.  More specific states of activity are differentiating within the wear category, while discrimination between the study having ended or not is differentiating within the non-wear category as shown in List 1 below.

Two digital filters (moving summers) are used to prepare the signal data for classification.  The first filter sums count values over a sliding window of 15 minutes, while the second uses a sliding window of five minutes to calculate sums of the adjacent five minutes (i.e. 2.5 minutes before and 2.5 following each adjacent sample).  These filters are applied separately (i.e. separate copies of the input data are used) to produce the long sum result and the short sum results respectively.  The long sum result is less susceptible to variation and gives a more global view, while the short sum is useful for determining more local, isolated periods of activity or inactivity and used here to differentiate sleep between NREM and REM.  It could also be extended to increase classification diversity of activity during wakefulness.

Candidate non-wear and wear activity is split at a threshold of 1 count per minute for 15 minutes; which is 15 in the case of the long filter.  Again, if the sum of 15 minutes of count activity (60 samples per minute) is less than 15, then the activity is defined as candidate non-wear.  

Non-wear

Candidate non-wear activity that is separated by less than fours is first merged to a form a continuous, unbroken chain of candidate non-wear activity.  After this, any candidate non-wear activity lasting more than four hours is categorized as non-wear.  

Study over

A majority of non-wear activity for 12 or more consecutive hours is classified study over.  This majority is determined by  grouping non-wear activity that falls within six hours of other non-wear activity and testing if the result exceeds 12 hours.  Thus, any interim gaps previously categorized as wear are considered as non-wear to allow for the possibility of spurious accelerometer readings do to bumps in the road or a person inadvertently bumping the accelerometers while it is still on, in storage, before its data is transferred to disk.   Any remaining activity that follows the classified study over period which is less than fours prior to the study finishing (e.g. again, there is a jerk of activity that prevents the study over from extending to the end) is further classified as study over.  Only one study over period is allowed.  In the case of more than one study over period, the one occurring last in the study is kept.  In some cases, a study has more than 12 hours of non-use during the middle of the study.  These periods remain classified as non-use.

Wear

Remaining activity, not classified as non-wear, is categorized as wear; the device is turned-on and is being worn by the participant.  The wear category is further differentiated between two more categories: wake and sleep.

Wake

The cutoff between awake and sleep is set to 1 count per second and is applied to the long sum (i.e. 900, or 1 count per second for 15 minutes at 60 seconds per minute).  Awake activity in excess of 10 counts per second is classified as active, otherwise it is classified inactive.  

Sleep

Activity less than 1 count per second, during wear, is classified sleep. 

An estimate of the type of sleep is next made.  Currently, the types of sleep estimated include: nap and prolonged sleep.  Within prolonged sleep, estimates are made between rapid-eye-movement sleep (possible REM) and non-REM sleep (possible NREM).

Prolonged sleep is defined as a minimum of 4 hours of consecutive sleep sections with fewer than two hours of interruption between sleep classifications.  Concisely, activity first classified as sleep is merged with other sleep classifications that are within 2 hours of each other.  Newly joined sleep sections that exceed 4 hours of duration are then classified as sleep period.  The rules allow overall identification of periods of sleep, which can be further examined for classification of sleep type (e.g. REM vs NREM) even though there may be periods of interruption during the sleep period (e.g. a child stirs, wakes up and/or needs to use the restroom).

Possible REM sleep is defined during prolonged sleep as short non-wear activity (occurrences where the short sum is less than the 1 count per minute threshold) that, after being merged within 5 minutes of related non-wear activity, exceeds 20 minutes.  Thus, REM sleep requires a minimum of 20 no-activity minutes during prolonged sleep.

Possible NREM sleep is is prolonged sleep not defined as possible REM; prolonged sleep not containing 20 or more minutes of merged, candidate non-wear activity.
